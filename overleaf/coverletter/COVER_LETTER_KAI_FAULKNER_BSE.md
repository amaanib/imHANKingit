Dear Professor Eeckhout,
I am writing to apply for the predoctoral research assistant position on the ERC project on Macro Market Power and Distribution. I am a current Masters in Economics student at the Barcelona School of Economics, having previously graduated from the University of Warwick in Philosophy, Politics and Economics with First Class Honours. My interest in this position stems directly from my undergraduate dissertation, which was inspired by the findings of De Loecker, Eeckhout and Unger (2020) on the macroeconomic implications of rising market power and sought to investigate whether similar dynamics could be observed in the United Kingdom.
The dissertation investigated how markups affect the pass-through of supply shocks to producer prices. I built a panel dataset of 281,642 firm-year observations by extracting, cleaning, deflating and merging 18 separate annual cross-sections of financial accounts from the Fame database, matching firms to 2-digit UK SIC industry codes, and trimming and imputing where necessary. I then estimated industry-level markups using the production function approach of De Ridder et al. (2024) with a translog functional form and the two-stage GMM procedure of De Loecker and Warzynski (2012), and used these in a two-way fixed effects model to test whether high-markup industries asymmetrically dampen the pass-through of oil supply shocks. The results replicated the dynamics found in the US and France by Kharroubi et al. (2023) and Arquié and Thié (2023). In the course of this work I also engaged with COMPUSTAT and the US markup literature at some length, so the datasets listed in the posting are not unfamiliar to me.
Separately, in my Applied Macroeconomics course with Thijs van Rens, I designed an original 3-state Diamond-Mortensen-Pissarides model with heterogeneous human capital and public/private sector differentiation. This was not a textbook replication; I specified the model from scratch, with multiple worker types and sector-specific matching functions, and solved for equilibrium in the frictional labour market. I mention this because the listing emphasises knowledge of search and matching theory, and this project gave me a working understanding of how these models are constructed and solved, which I think would transfer well to the computational and theoretical modelling described in the role.
My masters thesis involves building a heterogeneous agent New Keynesian model for monetary policy transmission in Brazil. Much of the work so far has been data infrastructure: constructing a classification pipeline implementing the Kaplan-Violante-Weidner (2014) hand-to-mouth taxonomy on Brazilian household survey microdata, and harmonising variable definitions across two surveys with different coding schemes to build demographic type shares for the model. On the computational side, I have experience with MATLAB from coursework at Warwick, where I used Dynare to solve DSGE models, and I am independently working through value function iteration and numerical methods as part of the thesis. I am also proficient in Stata, Python and R.
I recently received a 9.5 in Topics in Applied Economics I: Development Economics, a PhD-level course at UPF, and I am taking Advanced Techniques in Applied Economics as an MRes elective. Excelling in PhD-level coursework has strengthened my intention to pursue a PhD after gaining further research experience, and I believe this predoctoral position would be an ideal setting in which to do so.
